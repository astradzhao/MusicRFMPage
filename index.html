Abstract

We introduce our method, a framework for controllable music generation that adapts Recursive Feature Machines (RFMs) to steer a frozen MusicGen-Large model via activation-space interventions. Rather than optimizing at decode time or training auxiliary discriminators, our method derives orthogonal, eigenvalue-ranked concept directions from the Average Gradient Outer Product (AGOP) of lightweight layerwise probes, then injects these directions into the model’s residual stream with forward hooks.

We contribute:

  • Layer-based control with top-K and exponential weighting (with dropout) across all 48 decoder blocks.
  • Time-based schedules (linear/logistic rise, exponential decay, sinusoidal, and sparse random application) for modulating steering strength during generation.
  • Multi-direction control that supports simultaneous or staggered enforcement of attributes (e.g., tempo and chord structure).

Using the SynTheory dataset, we show that RFM probes achieve competitive classification accuracy compared to feedforward probes while yielding interpretable, steerable axes. When applied at inference, these directions bias generations toward target concepts while preserving text adherence.

In our primary analysis of RFM steering (control coefficients between 0.15 and 0.60, stochastic application probability 0.3, exponential layer dropout with parameters w₀=1 and κ=0.95), CLAP alignment remains within ~0.02 of the unsteered baseline, while probe-based concept accuracy increases monotonically with control strength (for example, note control accuracy rising from 0.23 to 0.82). Under time-based control, the probe softmax of the target note class faithfully follows the prescribed schedules, confirming precise temporal steering.

We also perform extensive ablations on pooling, coefficients, and time-based control on the notes category in SynTheory, chosen for its reliable measurement of accuracy.
